The 3-2-1 backup rule serves as a fundamental strategy for data protection and resilience against disaster.

Core Principles:

    Three copies of data: Maintain the primary working copy plus at least two separate backups to ensure recovery if one fails.
    Two different media types: Store backups on varied technologies (such as solid-state drives, hard disk drives, network-attached storage, or cloud services) to prevent systemic hardware failures.
    One offsite copy: Keep at least one backup in a geographically distant location to protect against localized disasters like fires, floods, or power outages.

Additional Resiliency Layers:

    One immutable or airgapped copy: Utilize storage that cannot be overwritten or altered to defend against ransomware. Immutable backups prevent modification once written, while airgapped systems are physically or logically disconnected to remain isolated from threats.
    Zero errors through testing: Regularly test backup recovery procedures. Simply having data is insufficient; verifying that it is usable and recoverable is essential to avoid complete loss during a crisis.

Strategic Considerations:

    Encryption: Protect sensitive information by encrypting all backups to prevent unauthorized access.
    Availability and Downtime: High availability targets, often expressed in "nines" (e.g., 99.999%), come with significant cost. Organizations must balance the investment in robust backup infrastructure against the potential business impact of downtime, as even small percentages of outages can lead to substantial interruptions.
